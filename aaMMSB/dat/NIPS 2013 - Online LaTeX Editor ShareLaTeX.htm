The posterior distribution over latent variables $p( {\pi_{1:N}}, z, {\beta_{1:K}} |
y,x,\alpha, \hat{\eta}, \eta, \delta)$ lets us form a predictive distribution over unseen
links, attributes and measure latent network properties of the observed data. As exact
inference of the posterior distribution is intractable, we use variational inference. The
variational inference algorithm is a coordinate ascent algorithm which in the context of
MMSB iterates between analyzing all $O(N^2)$ node pairs and updating the community
memberships of the $N$ nodes. Similar to the a-MMSB approach, we derive a stochastic
variational inference algorithm that interleaves subsampling the network and estimating its
community structure. This avoids the per-iteration $O(N^2)$ computation and allows us to
scale to large networks. Additionally, it allows gives us the opportunity to evaluate the
influence of different sampling strategies.
\label{gen_inst}
\subsection{Variational Inference in aa-MMSB}
In mean-field variational inference, Jensen's inequality is used to lower bound the log
likelihood of the observations, and each hidden variable is endowed with its own
distribution and its own variational parameter. The goal is to define a family of
distributions over the hidden variables and find a member of that family that is closest to
the posterior in terms of KL divergence. The joint distribution for the observed data and
the hidden variables given model parameters is given in Equation \ref{joint} below.
\begin{equation}
\begin{split}
\label{joint}
p(y, x, \pi, z, \beta | \alpha, \hat{\eta}, \eta, \delta)
&= \\
\prod_k p(\beta_k | \hat{\eta}) \prod_= p(\pi_p | &\alpha) \prod_{a, b} p(y_{ab} | z_{a
    \rightarrow b}, z_{a\leftarrow b}, \beta) p(z_{a \rightarrow b} | \pi_a) p(z_{a
    \leftarrow b} | \pi_b) \prod_p p(x_p | \bar{z_p}, \eta, \delta)
\end{split}
\end{equation}
Our model expands on a-MMSB by introducing a distribution on the attributes of the nodes.
The distribution of these attributes is highlighted below, where $x_{p, i}$ is the $i^{th}$
features of node $x_p$. In addition, $d^G$ denotes the number of Gaussian (real-valued)
features, $d^B$ denotes the number of Bernoulli (binary-valued) features. $\bar{z_p}$ is as
given in Equation \ref{zbar}.
